GRAPH TOPOLOGY & PATH ANALYSIS MODULE_V1.0
DOMAIN SPECIFIC ANALYSIS

Goal: Transforming structured and semistructured schemas and data formats into a homogeneous graph. KGs transform scattered data into a unified map.

  • Conductivity (Conductance): Low Value: Indicates a distinct, "tight-knit" community with few links to the outside world. This suggests a highly independent and well-defined functional module. High Value: Indicates a "leaky" or well-integrated group that shares many connections with the wider network, making it difficult to isolate as a separate entity.
  • Intensity (Density): High Value: Reflects a cohesive module where nodes utilize a high percentage of their potential internal connections, indicating frequent and intense collaboration. Low Value: Represents a sparse or loose pathway where interactions are rare relative to the total possible connections, suggesting a weakly interactive group.
  • Reachability: High Value: Suggests an integrated "Giant Component" where signals or information can traverse the majority of the network without hitting structural dead ends. Low Value: Indicates a fragmented or "shattered" system composed of isolated islands, where information is effectively trapped within disconnected silos.
PATHS AND METAPATHS

Path: A specific set of steps to get from A -> B.

Metapath: An established path pattern that showcases some phenomena ‘x’.

Path Count: Calculates the number of ways phenomena “x” can be arrived at.

THE MATHEMATICS: PDP AND DWPC

The Path-Degree Product (PDP):

$$PDP(path) = \prod_{d \in D_{path}} d^{-w}$$

For a single specific path, you look at every node's degree (number of connections) along that path. You raise each degree to the power of -w (the damping exponent) and multiply them together. Logic: Because the exponent is negative, a higher degree ($d$) results in a smaller PDP. A path through a "hub" node (like a very common gene) is worth less than a path through a "exclusive" node.

The Degree-Weighted Path Count (DWPC):

$$DWPC_m(s, t) = \sum_{path \in Paths_m(s, t)} PDP(path)$$

To calculate the DWPC, you first find every unique path that flows through your metapath pattern, then you sum their individual PDP values. It tells you not just how many ways there are to get from A to B, but how meaningful or specific those connections are.

WHY AND WHEN IS THIS USEFUL?

In large knowledge graphs like Hetionet, some nodes are "celebrities"—they connect to almost everything (e.g., a common side effect like "nausea" or a well-studied gene).

Problem with PC: If you use basic Path Count, any metapath involving a celebrity node will have a massive score simply because that node is a massive hub, not because the relationship is meaningful.

The DWPC Solution: It downweights these "well-known nodes." It prioritizes paths that are specific and rare.

When to use it:

  • Target Discovery: When you want to find a drug-disease link that isn't just "this drug causes nausea" (which applies to almost every drug).
  • Recommendation Systems: When you want to suggest a niche movie a user will love, rather than just suggesting The Avengers because everyone has seen it.
  • Link Prediction: When you need to determine if a connection is biologically significant or just a byproduct of a node being over-researched.
