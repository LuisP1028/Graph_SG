GRAPH MODEL DESIGN HEURISTICS MODULE_V1.0
MODEL DESIGN QUESTIONS

Goal: When I am designing a graph model, I am trying to answer the following questions:

  • Node Quantity: How many different types of nodes are present?
  • Node Attributes: What kind of properties do these nodes have?
  • Unique Identifiers: Which property would you use to store the unique identifier of nodes?
  • Relationship Types: What type of relationship are present?
  • Domain Accuracy: Is a single relationship type enough to accurately describe your domain?
  • Semantics: Does the relationship direction hold any semantic value?
  • Qualification: How do properties qualify or quantify relationships?
DATABASE VS PROJECTION

Stored Graph: Think of your database as a giant, messy warehouse of information

Projecting a graph: Selecting a specific "subset" or "view" of your data and loading it into the computer's RAM so an algorithm can work with it.

GRAPH TOPOLOGY AND FEATURES MODULE_V1.0
DIRECTIONALITY DYNAMICS

Undirected Graph: In an undirected graph, relationships are binary: you are either connected or you aren't. There is only one possible link between any two nodes.

Directed Graph: In a directed graph, the "outline" is more complex because directionality adds layers. You have to account for:

  • Asymmetry: A can point to B without B pointing back.
  • Reciprocity: A and B can both point to each other (two distinct links).
NODE FEATURES & ANALYSIS

Goal: Node Features are quantitative properties derived from a node's position and behavior.

  • Betweenness: Measures control and flow. In a corporate email network, this is the project manager connecting different departments. In global logistics, it is a major shipping canal like Suez.
  • Degree: Measures immediate connectivity. In social media, this is a user’s follower count. In a power grid, it’s a substation with numerous outgoing lines.
  • Graphlet Signature: Measures local geometry. In biology, specific patterns of protein interactions distinguish "master switches" from "worker" proteins. In banking, these patterns help flag automated bot-like transaction rings.
  • Closeness: Measures reach. In epidemiology, this identifies "super-spreaders" who can infect a population in the fewest steps.
